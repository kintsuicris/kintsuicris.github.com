Endorsement

Endorsement refers to the backing, support, or approval of a product by a third party, such as a celebrity (e.g., Friedman and Friedman 1979; Hung 2013), a product category expert (Hovland 1953), a typical customer (e.g., Tom et al. 1992), or an organization (e.g., Dean and Biswas 2001; Lafferty and Goldsmith 1999; Woo, Fock, and Hui 2006).

Celebrity Endorsement

  1. Affect-transfer Model

    Higher likeability/familiarity/similarity is associated with greater effectiveness in terms of message persuasiveness (McGuire 1985).

    Attractiveness of a communicator determines the effectiveness of persuasive communication through a process called identification which is assumed to occur when information from an attractive source is accepted as a result of desire to identify with such endorsers (Cohen and Golden 1972).

    Problem: Not even one celebrity is liked by everyone.

  2. Meaning-transfer Model

    Celebrity endorsers bring their own symbolic meanings to the endorsement process. The cultural meanings residing in a celebrity go beyond the person and are passed on to the products (McCracken 1989; Brierley 1995).

    Consumers are able to recognize symbolic properties of products and transfer them into meanings for themselves (Levy 1959).

    The movement of meanings from consumer goods to the individual consumer is accomplished through the efforts of consumers who must take possession of these meanings and put them to work in the construction of their self-image.

    Sometimes a negative celebrity have positive effect.

    Problem: Celebrities have different meanings in different periods.

  3. Mindset-transfer Model

    While traditional models suggested that value, and affect can be transferred from the endorser to the product through endorsement, a mindset-transfer model explores the possibility that evaluative procedures can also be transferred.
    Evaluation Strategy

    A conceptually similar mindset is activated
    Using background information to evaluate the wine
